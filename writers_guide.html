Screenplay Writer's Guide

The Typographical Landscape & formatting standards of modern screenplays.

The screenplay is a highly specialized, minimalist literary form designed to serve as a functional, technical blueprint for a collaborative, visual medium. Unlike novels or theatrical plays—which rely on internal monologues, extensive descriptions, or stage-oriented blocking—a screenplay must convey its story entirely through what can be physically seen and heard on screen.

This strict demand for visual externalization has resulted in a rigid typographical ecosystem that has remained virtually unchanged since the era of mechanical typewriters.

Within this structural framework, every element on the page is assigned a specific spatial coordinate, font weight, and programmatic function.2 To comprehend how a screenwriter controls the pacing and visual flow of a film, one must understand how to master the primary formatting elements:

  • Master Scene Heading
  • Action
  • Character Cue
  • Dialogue
  • Parentheticals
  • Transitions
  • Shot

And how they interact under the industry-accepted layout known as the American Screenwriting Standard (or master-scene format).

1. General Page Layout, Geometry, and Typography

The layout rules of the American Screenwriting Standard are mathematically fixed to preserve a critical industry calculation: one page of formatted script equals roughly one minute of screentime.

If margins, font, or line spacing are modified, this page-to-minute ratio breaks down, impacting scheduling, budgeting, and production planning.3

Paper and Binding Specifications

For hard copies, the physical layout demands strict adherence to traditional materials:

Paper Size: Standard US Letter (8.5 inches by 11 inches).
Paper Stock: Plain white, 20 lb. bond paper.
Printing: Single-sided only.
Binding: Three-hole punched paper bound with exactly two brass brads (fastened in the top and bottom holes, leaving the center hole empty). Avoid folders, fancy covers, or plastic bindings.4

Typographical Standards

Font Type and Size: Standard Courier 12-point (10-pitch). Using proportional fonts (like Times New Roman) or other sizing immediately marks a script as amateurish. Monospaced fonts ensure that every letter and space occupies the exact same horizontal width, which is the foundational basis for the one-minute-per-page calculation.
Line Spacing: The document is single-spaced internally, with double-spacing (one blank line) separating distinct scene elements.
Page Volume: This results in approximately 55 vertical lines of text per page (excluding headers), up to a maximum of 60 lines.6
Page Numbering: Placed in the top right-hand corner of the page, flush to the right margin, exactly 0.5 inches from the top edge, followed by a period (e.g., 2.). The title page and the very first page of the script are left unnumbered. Numbering officially begins on the third physical sheet of the document (page 2).

3. The Six Core Elements of Screenplay Format

The American Screenwriting Standard structures all narrative actions and dialogue blocks into six major formatting elements:

A. Scene Headings (Sluglines)

Located at the beginning of each scene, this line establishes the environment.9 It must be written in ALL CAPS and contains three distinct components:

1. Interior or Exterior Designation: INT. (interior) or EXT. (exterior).
  • For scenes transitioning between both (e.g., inside a moving car), use INT./EXT. or I./E..
2. Location: The specific physical setting (e.g., KEVIN'S HOUSE - KITCHEN).
  • Sub-locations can be clarified with hyphens.
3. Time of Day: Standard designations are DAY or NIGHT. Special modifiers like MORNING, AFTERNOON, SAME, or CONTINUOUS are acceptable when continuity is key.
INT. KEVIN'S HOUSE - KITCHEN - DAY

B. Action (Screen Direction)

This narrative block describes the physical environment, character movements, and auditory events.

Always written in the present tense.
Limited strictly to what can be physically seen and heard by an audience.
Character Introductions: When a character is introduced for the first time:
  • Their name is capitalized in ALL CAPS, followed by their age in parentheses and a brief description.
The back door swings open. LIAM (30s), a disheveled accountant, stumbles inside clutching a blood-stained folder.

C. Character Cue

Before any dialogue is spoken, the character’s speaking identity must be called out.

The name is centered, written in ALL CAPS, and positioned at the 3.7" to 4.1" margin.
Character names must remain entirely consistent throughout the script to avoid confusing the breakdown services.4

Extensions: Placed directly adjacent to the character name in parentheses3:

(V.O.) for Voice-Over (narration, thoughts, radio voices, or telephone speakers on the other end of a call).
(O.S.) for Off-Screen (when a character is physically present in the scene's location but is speaking from outside the camera's visual frame).
(CONT'D) when a character’s dialogue block is briefly interrupted by an action line and then continues.

D. Parentheticals (Wrylies)

These are short, lowercase directions enclosed in parentheses, placed directly beneath the Character Cue and before their dialogue, or inside a dialogue block.

Indented at 3.1" left and 3.1" right.3
Used sparingly and only to clarify the tone or emotional delivery when the text itself could be misread (e.g., (sarcastic) or (whispering)).
  • They should not be used to micromanage actor movements.
LIAM (whispering) We don't have much time.

E. Dialogue

The actual spoken lines of a character.

Formatted in sentence case.1
Indented to a left margin of 2.5" to 2.7" and a right margin of 1.5" to 2.4".3
If a line trails off, use an ellipsis (...). If a line is abruptly cut off, end the speech with a double-hyphen (--).

F. Transitions

Editing directions indicating how to transition between scenes, positioned flush to the right margin at the 6.0" mark.3

Written in ALL CAPS and end with a colon (e.g., CUT TO:, DISSOLVE TO:, SMASH TO:).
In spec scripts, transitions are kept to an absolute minimum or omitted entirely, as editing styles are determined in post-production.

4. Contextualizing the Shot Element within Narrative Blueprinting

A shot heading is a formatting device designed to alter the focal point of the reader's eye within an already established scene.8

While a Scene Heading changes the physical space or time, a Shot Heading changes the physical framing or camera perspective within that same location.11

Rules of Shot Headings

Margins: Formatted flush left (or aligned to 1.7" left margin).
Casing: Written in ALL CAPS.4
Spacing: Preceded by a blank line and followed by a blank line. (Note: Some classic manuals like SimplyScripts mention two blank lines preceding a shot, but a single blank line is standard in contemporary software).8

Common Shot Heading Types8:

ANGLE ON -- 8
EXTREME CLOSE UP ON -- 8
WIDER ANGLE -- 12
REVERSE ANGLE -- 8
INSERT -- (specifically used to draw attention to small details, text, or props that must be legible to the audience).8

5. Special Formatting Situations

Many narrative scenarios require complex structures beyond basic dialogue.

The American Screenwriting Standard outlines precise protocols for telephone calls, text messages, flashbacks, and montages.

A. Telephone Conversations

There are three industry-approved methods to format telephone exchanges:

Method 1: One-Sided Call (Only One Character Seen and Heard)

Treat the scene like normal dialogue. The pauses where the other character speaks are indicated by physical action lines, avoiding repetitive "beat" parentheticals.

INT. KITCHEN - DAY Clara frantically scrubs the blood-soaked tile. The phone RINGS. She answers it. CLARA Hello? (listening) No, this is she. Who is this? Clara stops scrubbing, her posture stiffening. CLARA (CONT'D) You're lying. He's at work.

Method 2: Two-Sided Call with Voice-Over (One Character Seen, Both Heard)

The character on the other end is heard but not physically seen. Their Character Cue must utilize the (V.O.) extension to represent the electronic transmission of their voice.

INT. KITCHEN - DAY Clara answers the ringing phone. CLARA Hello? SHADOWY FIGURE (V.O.) Clara. I have your husband.

Method 3: Intercut Call (Both Characters Seen and Heard)

To show both characters in their respective locations cutting back and forth, establish both locations first, then insert an INTERCUT command.

This allows the writer to list the dialogue continuously without writing new scene headings for every single line.

INT. CLARA'S KITCHEN - DAY Clara answers the ringing phone. CLARA Hello? INT. WALL STREET OFFICE - SAME TIME A SHADOWY FIGURE sits behind a mahogany desk, holding the receiver. SHADOWY FIGURE Is Clara Evans available? INTERCUT - PHONE CONVERSATION CLARA This is she.

B. Text Messaging

Because texting is silent, formatting it as spoken dialogue can confuse the actor or sound department.

The industry utilizes three main formatting approaches:

Method 1: Within Action Description (Best for Quick, Single Texts)

Describe the character looking at the screen and quote the text directly in the action line.

Erik pulls out his phone. A new text from "Wife" reads: "Pick up milk." Erik sighs and pockets the phone.

Method 2: Insert Shot (Best for Crucial Visual Reveals)

If the precise wording of a text is a major plot point, use an INSERT shot to force the reader to see the screen.

INT. ERIK'S OFFICE - DAY Erik's phone vibrates on the desk. He looks at it. INSERT PHONE SCREEN Incoming call from "WIFE." BACK TO SCENE Erik silences the call and returns to typing.

Method 3: Dialogue Formatting with (TEXT) Extension (Best for Back-and-Forth Conversations)

Format the texts like spoken lines but append the (TEXT) extension next to the character cue.

INT. ERIK'S OFFICE - DAY Erik types rapidly on his phone. ERIK (TEXT) I'll be late tonight, sorry! WIFE (TEXT) Again? Dinner is cold.

C. Flashbacks

Flashbacks must be formatted so that the production crew knows exactly when the story moves in and out of the timeline.

Method 1: Integrated Scene Heading Modifier (Best for Full-Scene Flashbacks)

Append - FLASHBACK directly to the end of the Scene Heading. The returning scene must append - BACK TO PRESENT DAY or - PRESENT DAY.

EXT. TRAIN TRACKS - DAY - FLASHBACK A ten-year-old Barry sprints along the tracks, laughing. INT. Barry's OFFICE - NIGHT - BACK TO PRESENT DAY The adult Barry (40s) stares out the window at the rainy city skyline.

Method 2: Subheading Indicators (Best for Flashbacks within a Scene)

Insert BEGIN FLASHBACK and END FLASHBACK as distinct subheadings.

INT. Barry's OFFICE - NIGHT Barry stares at a framed photograph of a steam engine. BEGIN FLASHBACK: Ten-year-old Barry stands on a platform as a massive train roars past. END FLASHBACK Barry wipes a tear from his cheek and sits at his desk.

D. Montages

A montage is a series of rapid, thematic visual fragments spanning multiple locations or times.

Begin with a capitalized subheading: MONTAGE -.
List each brief visual snippet on a separate line, typically using double-hyphens (--).
Conclude with a returning subheading: END MONTAGE or BACK TO SCENE.
MONTAGE - LIAM ELUDES THE POLICE -- Liam ducks into a narrow, trash-filled alleyway. -- He sprints across a crowded subway platform, blending into the crowd. -- In a public restroom, he dyes his hair brown in the sink. END MONTAGE

6. The Revision and Production Process: Spec Scripts vs. Production Drafts

Understanding the distinction between a Spec Script and a Production Draft (Shooting Script) is critical to professional industry survival.

Spec Scripts: The Story Catalyst

A "spec" script is written on speculation (uncommissioned and unsold). Its primary audience is the Script Reader, Producer, and Talent Agent.5

Focuses entirely on pacing, narrative flow, character development, and readability.15
OMITS scene numbers.16
AVOIDS technical camera directions (e.g., PAN TO, TRACKING SHOT, CRANE UP).
  • Writing camera directions "directs from the page" and is considered an intrusion upon the director's creative authority.17

Production Drafts: The Technical Blueprint

Once a script is purchased and budgeted, it transitions into a Shooting Script.14

It is now a technical scheduling document used by the Director, Assistant Directors, Cinematographer, and Crew.15

Scene numbers are added to both the left and right margins.
Scene numbers on the left are aligned at 0.75" to 1.0", while the right number is placed at 7.4" (or 1.0" from the right edge).3
Camera and editing directions are added to guide visual setups.15

Revisions and Scene Management Rules

During production, revisions are made to locked scripts. To prevent the entire shooting schedule from breaking, page numbers and scene numbers cannot simply be reordered.

Instead, the industry uses specific mathematical scene indicators:

1. Inserting a Scene: If a new scene is added between Scene 10 and Scene 11, the new scene is labeled with a letter suffix: 10B.
  • Further additions in that same slot become 10C, 10D, etc..
  • Note that letters are never prefixed (e.g., I8 is unacceptable).
2. Moving a Scene: If Scene 44 is moved between Scenes 39 and 40, Scene 44 is given the new number 39B.
  • At its original location (Scene 44), the heading is kept to lock the numbering but is renamed: 44. SCENE OMITTED - moved to 39B.
3. Deleting (Omit) a Scene:
  • To delete a scene entirely without throwing off the downstream numbering, the original scene number is preserved.
  • The body text is deleted and the slugline is updated to read: 44. SCENE OMITTED.

By adhering to these standard layouts, a screenwriter demonstrates professional competence, ensuring their work is readable, schedulable, and ready for production.
